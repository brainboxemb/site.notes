# Woodworking Videos

This page contains useful woodworking videos for building and finishing the HUB75 display enclosure.

## Finishing Plywood Edges with Solid Oak

- [YouTube — Using oak on the exposed edges of plywood](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qbDb97wQ-r0)

This video shows how solid oak can be applied to the exposed edges of plywood.

This may be useful for the HUB75 display enclosure because plywood edges remain visible after cutting. Adding a strip of solid oak can provide a cleaner and more durable edge than filling or painting the plywood edge directly.

Possible applications include:

- The front edge around the display
- Visible outside corners
- The top and side edges of the enclosure

## Pocket-Hole Joinery

- [YouTube — Pocket-hole joinery](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vUmbP6dMJHQ&t=629s)

This part of the video explains the use of pocket holes for joining wooden panels.

A pocket-hole jig drills an angled hole into one panel. A screw is then driven through this hole into the adjoining panel.

This technique may be useful for assembling the display enclosure because the screws can be positioned on the inside of the cabinet.

Points to consider include:

- Correct jig setting for the panel thickness
- Correct screw length
- Accurate alignment before fastening
- Whether the pocket holes remain accessible during assembly
- Whether additional glue is required

## Simple Dado Jig

- [YouTube — Simple dado jig](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zvqUyk6f13I&t=12s)

This video shows a simple adjustable router jig for cutting dados that match the thickness of the material.

A dado is a groove cut across a wooden panel. Another panel can fit into this groove, creating a stronger and more accurately aligned joint.

This could be useful for:

- Positioning internal partitions
- Mounting support panels
- Holding the rear panel
- Aligning shelves or electronics mounting plates

The jig is adjusted using the actual material, which helps compensate for plywood that is slightly thinner than its nominal thickness.

## Simple Crosscut Sled

- [YouTube — Simple crosscut sled](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0sdm_Ow-BTU&t=15s)

This video shows how to build a simple crosscut sled for a table saw.

A crosscut sled supports the workpiece while it is moved through the saw blade. It can help produce repeatable square cuts and provides more support than using only the mitre gauge.

This may be useful when cutting enclosure parts such as:

- Side panels
- Top and bottom panels
- Internal supports
- Solid-wood edge strips

Important points include:

- Keeping the rear fence square to the blade
- Ensuring that the runners move smoothly without excessive play
- Keeping hands outside the blade area
- Clearly marking where the blade exits the rear fence
